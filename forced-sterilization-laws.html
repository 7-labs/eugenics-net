Foundational article

Forced Sterilization Laws

Forced sterilization turned eugenic ideology into bodily coercion. It used law, medicine, and institutions to deny reproductive autonomy to targeted people.

Editorial owner: Eugenics History & Bioethics Project Last updated: May 30, 2026 Topics: forced sterilization, law, reproductive rights
Content warning: Discusses coercive sterilization, reproductive violence, disability discrimination, racism, and institutional control.

Project Position

This article treats forced sterilization as a human-rights violation, not as a public-health tool.

What Forced Sterilization Means

Forced sterilization is a medical or surgical intervention that prevents reproduction without free and informed consent. In eugenic policy, it was often presented as protection of the public, relief for institutions, or improvement of the population.

Those justifications hid the central reality: targeted people lost bodily autonomy.

Legal and Institutional Machinery

Sterilization laws gave authority to courts, boards, hospitals, prisons, and state institutions. Decisions could be made through paperwork, hearings, diagnoses, or administrative labels that people had little power to challenge.

The targets were often people already under state or institutional control: disabled people, institutionalized people, incarcerated people, poor people, and racialized communities.

Consent and Coercion

Ethical medical care depends on informed, voluntary consent. Eugenic sterilization violated that principle. Even when a form appeared to record consent, the surrounding conditions could involve pressure, misinformation, dependency, language barriers, or fear of losing services.

This is why modern discussions must distinguish medical sterilization chosen by a patient from coercive sterilization imposed by institutions.

Continuing Legacy

The legacy includes trauma, family separation, distrust of medical institutions, legal claims, apology campaigns, and compensation efforts. A responsible archive should treat survivors and affected communities as central historical actors, not as footnotes to policy.

Teaching and Discussion Questions

  1. Why were people in institutions especially vulnerable to sterilization laws?
  2. How did medical language make coercion appear legitimate?
  3. What does reproductive autonomy require?
