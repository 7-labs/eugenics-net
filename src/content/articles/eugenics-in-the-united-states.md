## More Than a Fringe Movement

Eugenics in the United States involved universities, state institutions, public-health departments, courts, philanthropies, schools, and advocacy organizations. It was not only a set of private prejudices. It was an institutional program that made hierarchy administratively legible.

## Sterilization Laws

Many U.S. states passed laws authorizing involuntary sterilization. These laws often targeted people labeled disabled, mentally ill, poor, criminal, socially dependent, or otherwise "unfit." The categories were shaped by institutional power and social prejudice, not by a reliable science of human worth.

The impact fell heavily on people who had limited ability to resist: people in institutions, prisons, hospitals, and welfare systems, as well as racialized and poor communities.

## Immigration and National Identity

U.S. eugenicists also argued that immigration should be restricted to protect the nation's supposed hereditary stock. These arguments linked heredity, race, nationality, and social value. Immigration policy became one route through which scientific racism and eugenics entered national law.

## Public Health and Education

Eugenic ideas appeared in public-health campaigns, school materials, fairs, and social reform language. This matters because education can either challenge dehumanizing claims or quietly reproduce them. A teaching resource should therefore name the harm rather than treat eugenics as a neutral historical curiosity.

## Legacy

The legacy of U.S. eugenics includes survivor testimony, compensation campaigns, institutional reckoning, and continuing distrust of medical and public-health systems. A credible archive must center those harms, not only the institutions that produced them.
